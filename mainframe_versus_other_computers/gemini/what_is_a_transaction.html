I asked Gemini: "what is a transaction?"

A **transaction** is a sequence of one or more operations that are treated as a single, indivisible unit of work. This means that either all the operations within the transaction are successfully completed, or none of them are. If any part of the transaction fails, the entire transaction is rolled back, leaving the system in its original state as if the transaction never occurred. Think of it like an "all or nothing" deal. Here's a breakdown of the key characteristics often associated with transactions, especially in the context of data management systems (like databases): * **Atomicity:** This is the "all or nothing" property. The transaction is treated as a single, indivisible unit. * **Consistency:** A transaction takes the system from one valid state to another valid state. It ensures that data integrity rules and constraints are maintained. * **Isolation:** When multiple transactions are running concurrently, they should operate independently of each other. The intermediate state of one transaction should not be visible to other transactions, preventing data corruption or inconsistencies. * **Durability:** Once a transaction is successfully completed (committed), the changes to the data are permanent and will survive system failures (like power outages or crashes). **In simpler terms:** Imagine you're transferring money from your savings account to your checking account online. This involves two main operations: 1. Decreasing the balance in your savings account. 2. Increasing the balance in your checking account. This entire process should be treated as a single transaction. If the system successfully decreases your savings balance but fails to increase your checking balance (due to an error), the transaction should be rolled back, and your savings account should be restored to its original balance. This ensures that money isn't lost in transit and the system remains consistent. While the concept of transactions is most commonly discussed in the context of databases, the underlying principle of treating a series of actions as a single, reliable unit of work applies in other areas of software as well (as we discussed with non-database examples earlier). The goal is always to maintain data integrity and ensure reliable operations.
