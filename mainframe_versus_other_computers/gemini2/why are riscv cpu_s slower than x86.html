I asked Gemini "why are riscv cpu_s slower than x86"

It's not entirely accurate to say that RISC-V CPUs are inherently "slower" than x86 CPUs in a fundamental, theoretical sense. Both are instruction set architectures (ISAs), and the actual performance depends heavily on the microarchitecture (the specific design of the CPU) and the implementation (how that design is built on silicon).

However, in the current market, high-performance x86 CPUs generally outperform high-performance RISC-V CPUs. Here's why:

  1. Maturity and Investment:

    • x86: Intel and AMD have spent decades and billions of dollars optimizing x86 microarchitectures. They've poured resources into advanced techniques like deep pipelines, out-of-order execution, speculative execution, sophisticated branch prediction, large multi-level caches, and highly optimized compilers. This extensive R&D has led to highly refined and performant designs.

    • RISC-V: As a newer, open-source ISA, RISC-V is still relatively young in terms of high-performance implementations. While there are companies developing advanced RISC-V cores, they haven't had the same decades of iterative refinement and massive investment as Intel and AMD.

  1. Complexity of Design and Manufacturing:

    • x86: The x86 ISA is a Complex Instruction Set Computer (CISC) architecture.1 While modern x86 CPUs translate CISC instructions into simpler micro-operations internally (making them more RISC-like under the hood), the decoding logic for x86 instructions is still incredibly complex and requires significant silicon area and power. This complexity, while challenging, has been masterfully optimized over time to extract maximum performance.

    • RISC-V: RISC-V, as a Reduced Instruction Set Computer (RISC) architecture, has a simpler and more streamlined instruction set.2 This simplicity can lead to more power-efficient designs and easier implementation.3 However, achieving high performance often means executing more instructions to accomplish the same task as a complex x86 instruction. To compensate, RISC-V designs (like any high-performance CPU) need advanced microarchitectural features. Many current RISC-V implementations are focused on embedded systems and lower-power applications where simplicity and efficiency are prioritized over raw peak performance. High-performance features like deep out-of-order pipelines and large caches are starting to appear in RISC-V designs, but they are not as prevalent or as mature as in x86.

  1. Ecosystem and Software Optimization:

    • x86: x86 has a vast, mature software ecosystem with decades of optimization. Compilers, operating systems (like Windows), and applications have been heavily tuned to leverage x86-specific features and instructions (like AVX-512).

    • RISC-V: The software ecosystem for RISC-V is growing rapidly but is still less mature.4 While Linux support is improving, other operating systems like Windows have limited or no native support. Developers and compilers are still catching up in optimizing code specifically for various RISC-V implementations. The modularity of RISC-V, while an advantage for customization, can also lead to fragmentation if not managed well, potentially making software optimization more challenging across different RISC-V implementations.5

  1. Market Focus:

    • x86: Primarily targets high-performance desktops, laptops, and servers, where raw computational power is often the top priority, even at the cost of higher power consumption and complexity.6

    • RISC-V: Has a strong presence in embedded systems, IoT devices, and specialized accelerators, where efficiency, customizability, and lower licensing costs are key.7 While efforts are underway to push RISC-V into higher-performance segments, it's a newer focus for many developers.


In summary:

RISC-V isn't inherently slower, but the current state of its high-performance implementations lags behind the highly optimized and mature x86 designs due to differences in R&D investment, the complexity of existing designs, and the maturity of their respective ecosystems.8 As RISC-V continues to evolve and attract more investment, its performance gap with x86 in high-end applications is expected to narrow.
