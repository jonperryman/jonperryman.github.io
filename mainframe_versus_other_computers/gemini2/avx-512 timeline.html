I asked Gemini "avx-512 timeline"

AVX-512 (Advanced Vector Extensions 512) is a set of 512-bit SIMD (Single Instruction, Multiple Data) instruction set extensions for the x86 architecture.1 Its development and adoption have followed a notable timeline, primarily driven by Intel, with AMD joining later.2


Here's a timeline of AVX-512:

Intel's Development and Introduction:

  • July 2013: Intel formally proposed the AVX-512 instruction set, introducing a new EVEX prefix encoding to support the wider vectors and new features like mask registers.3 This marks the official public announcement and specification of the instruction set.

  • 2016:

    • June 2016: The first Intel processors to implement AVX-512 were the Xeon Phi x200 series (codenamed Knights Landing) co-processors.4 These were specialized many-core processors designed for high-performance computing (HPC) and scientific workloads, not general-purpose CPUs.5 They supported the AVX512F (Foundation) and AVX512CD (Conflict Detection) instruction subsets.6

  • 2017:

    • Q3 2017: AVX-512 was introduced into mainstream Intel server and high-end desktop (HEDT) processors with the Skylake-SP (Xeon Scalable) and Skylake-X architectures. This marked its availability in general-purpose CPUs. These processors generally supported AVX512F, AVX512CD, AVX512BW (Byte and Word), and AVX512DQ (Doubleword and Quadword) subsets, and AVX512VL (Vector Length).

    • Q4 2017: Knights Mill (Xeon Phi) further expanded AVX-512 support, adding AVX512_4VNNIW and AVX512_4FMAPS.

  • 2018:

    • Q3 2018: Cannon Lake (a limited consumer release) introduced AVX512IFMA and AVX512VBMI.

  • 2019:

    • Q2 2019: Cascade Lake (server/HEDT) added AVX512VNNI (Vector Neural Network Instructions) for AI/deep learning workloads.

    • Q3 2019: Ice Lake (server/some mobile) significantly expanded AVX-512 with additional subsets like AVX512VPOPCNTDQ, AVX512VBMI2, AVX512BITALG, and AVX512VPCLMULQDQ.

  • 2020:

    • Q2 2020: Cooper Lake (server) also included AVX512VNNI.

    • Q3 2020: Tiger Lake (mobile) brought AVX-512 to some mainstream mobile processors.

  • 2021:

    • Q1 2021: Rocket Lake (desktop) included AVX-512, making it available to a broader consumer desktop audience, but it was quickly overshadowed.

    • Q4 2021: Alder Lake (desktop/laptop) was a turning point. Due to its hybrid architecture (performance cores and efficiency cores), Intel largely fused off or disabled AVX-512 on most consumer Alder Lake parts to avoid complexities with instruction set availability across different core types.7 Some early batches or specific motherboard BIOS configurations might have allowed its enablement, but this was generally not officially supported.

  • 2023:

    • Q1 2023: Sapphire Rapids (4th Gen Xeon Scalable) fully embraced AVX-512 for server workloads, including AVX512_FP16 (Half-Precision Floating Point).8

    • July 2023: Intel announced AVX10, a new branding and instruction set architecture that aims to unify various AVX-512 subsets and ensure consistent availability across hybrid architectures (e.g., P-cores and E-cores) at different vector widths (128, 256, and 512-bit).9 AVX10 is intended to be the successor to AVX-512 for future Intel CPUs, with backward compatibility.10

  • Q4 2023: Emerald Rapids (5th Gen Xeon Scalable) continued full AVX-512 support.

  • 2024: Granite Rapids (6th Gen Xeon Scalable) is expected to be the first to implement AVX10.1, carrying forward the AVX-512 capabilities.

AMD's Introduction:

  • 2022:

    • Q3 2022: AMD introduced AVX-512 support with their Zen 4 architecture (Ryzen 7000 series desktop CPUs and EPYC 9004 series server CPUs).11 Zen 4's implementation initially "double-pumped" 512-bit operations through its 256-bit wide execution units. This meant instructions were supported, but throughput for 512-bit operations was effectively halved.

  • 2024:

    • August 2024 (expected/current): AMD's Zen 5 architecture (Ryzen 9000 series and new EPYC processors) significantly improves AVX-512 implementation by featuring full-width 512-bit execution units in its desktop and server parts, providing native 512-bit performance.12 Some mobile Zen 5 parts (like Strix Point) may still use the double-pumped approach.13


This timeline illustrates that while AVX-512 has been around for some time, its journey to broad availability and optimized performance on general-purpose CPUs has been gradual and, in the consumer space, has seen some divergence between Intel and AMD's strategies.
